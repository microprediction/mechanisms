A World Cup of Prediction Markets

Which nations actually built the machinery of betting, pricing, and forecasting?

The final

🇦🇺 Australia
🇺🇸 USA
→
🏆  your call

Australia built the machine — George Julius turned the parimutuel into a thundering acre of shafts, cables and epicyclic gears that tallied thousands of bets a second and flashed the live odds across the track: a room-sized real-time computer (1913) that beat the electronic kind to it by decades. America built the theory — from Bell Labs to the blockchain — though it dawdled in the tunnel: a couple of centuries of puritanical disapproval of betting kept it on the bench, after which it came on and never stopped scoring.

The road there (knockout recaps)

Quarter-finals

🇦🇺 Australia 3–1 🇯🇵 Japan. Julius opens with the totalisator, Hyndman adds a forecasting goal, and the Sydney Futures Exchange converts the first financial-futures contract launched outside the US (1979). Japan replies through the Dōjima Rice Exchange (Osaka, sanctioned 1730 — the first organised futures market), and run the hosts close with Amari's information geometry and Kiyosi Itô's stochastic calculus — the very maths the rest of the tournament is played on.

🇳🇱 Netherlands 2–1 🇩🇪 Germany. The Dutch press with the VOC and the Amsterdam bourse (1602, the first modern stock exchange) and the descending-price flower clock; Germany answers through Gneiting's strictly proper scoring rules and Selten's game theory, but the founders of the share market hold on.

🇺🇸 USA 5–0 🇮🇹 Italy. The American front line runs riot — Kelly's growth-optimal betting, Vickrey's second-price auction, the Iowa Electronic Markets, Hanson's LMSR, Vernon Smith's experimental double auction. Italy keep it dignified in possession through Cardano (the first treatise on games of chance), de Finetti (probability from coherent betting odds), and the Genoese lotto itself.

🇫🇷 France 2–1 🇬🇧 UK. Oller's pari-mutuel (he also founded the Moulin Rouge) and Bachelier's 1900 random walk do the scoring, but France's spine is deeper still: Pascal and Fermat invented expected value settling a 1654 gambling dispute, and Laplace marshals the probability behind them. They edge a stacked British side of Bayes, Ramsey, Keynes's beauty contest, Galton's vox populi, and Good & Turing at Bletchley. The result flatters nobody; the UK out-pass everyone and still go out.

Semi-finals

🇦🇺 Australia 1–0 🇳🇱 Netherlands. A Quiggin screamer — rank-dependent (anticipated) expected utility, the theory of how people overweight long-shot probabilities — settles it. Australia's theoretical goal of the tournament, and a direct hit on the favourite–longshot bias the Dutch never closed down.

🇺🇸 USA 3–2 🇫🇷 France. A classic. Savage, Arrow, and Fama's efficient markets for the US; Walras's imaginary auctioneer and Bachelier for France. The American bench — Pennock, Chen, Wortman Vaughan — proves decisive late on.

And where America was held back by puritanical predilections, Australia was propelled by the opposite one. Off the bench it brings two-up — the people's parimutuel, a bet on the spin of two coins with no inventor on record (you cannot patent a coin toss). It descends from the British "cross and pile" / "pitch and toss"; the convicts' fondness for it was already logged by New South Wales's first judge advocate in 1798, and it spread across the goldfields by the 1850s. It is the purest binary market in the tournament — and, fittingly, legal nationwide roughly one day a year.

Group stage (the nations who shaped the game and went home early)

  • 🇦🇹 Austria — Friedrich Hayek, whose 1945 "The Use of Knowledge in Society" argues that a market is an information-processing machine, its prices aggregating dispersed knowledge no central planner could ever gather: the intellectual charter behind every prediction market on this site. Harsh on the federation that wrote the constitution to exit in the group stage — and Hayek saw out his career playing for Britain at the LSE.
  • 🇮🇱 Israel — Kahneman & Tversky (prospect theory: how people really weight probabilities), Aumann (correlated equilibrium), and Bancor (the first deployed on-chain AMM, 2017). Out on goal difference; widely held to have been robbed.
  • 🇭🇺 Hungary — von Neumann (the minimax theorem, game theory), Harsanyi (games of incomplete information), Wald (sequential analysis). A golden generation that mostly suited up for other nations.
  • 🇷🇺 Russia — Kolmogorov (the 1933 axioms of probability — literally the rulebook), Markov (Markov chains), Kantorovich (linear programming / optimal allocation). Wrote the laws of the game, then declined to play the markets.
  • 🇨🇭 Switzerland — the Bernoullis of Basel (the law of large numbers; expected utility and the St Petersburg paradox), the Lausanne School stadium (where Walras and Pareto played), and the Ethereum Foundation in Zug. Neutral hosts who happen to own the ground.
  • 🇮🇪 Ireland — Adrian Raftery (Bayesian model averaging; co-author of the canonical proper-scoring-rules paper) and Intrade, the great election exchange (to 2013).
  • 🇪🇸 Spain — El Gordo, the Lotería de Navidad (first drawn 1812; the largest lottery in the world by total prize payout) — and a formal protest lodged over France's Catalan-born striker.
  • 🇳🇿 New Zealand — host of the very first automatic tote (Ellerslie, Auckland, 1913). The assist of the century.
  • 🇨🇦 Canada — keeps having its players poached: Vickrey (Victoria, BC), Tetlock (Toronto), and Myron Scholes (Timmins, Ontario, of Black–Scholes–Merton) all turned out for the USA.
  • 🇨🇳 China — keno and the "white-pigeon ticket"; the tale that a lottery helped fund the Great Wall is folklore, not fact, and the goal is disallowed.

Several of these squads also carry a lottery, which is worth pausing on because pooled-stakes gambling is the parimutuel's own ancestor: Augustus drew prizes by lot at Roman feasts; Genoa abstracted betting on the names of elected officials into the 5-of-90 lotto (early 17th century); Elizabeth I held England's first state lottery (drawn 1569); the Dutch Staatsloterij (1726) is often called the oldest still running; Spain's El Gordo (1812) is the biggest by total payout; and a 1612 lottery helped bail out the Jamestown colony. Different mechanism, same idea as this whole site — put the stakes in a pool and let a rule do the rest.

The laws of the game

The squads are the theorists and market-builders; these are the laws of motion they all play under, and the mathematicians who wrote them.

Martingale theory — the fair game

In an efficient market today's price already reflects the public information, so the best forecast of tomorrow's price is today's — a martingale, named for an 18th-century French betting strategy and given rigorous form by the American Joseph Doob in the 1950s:

$$\mathbb{E}[X_{n+1}\mid X_1,\dots,X_n]=X_n.$$

It is the bedrock under Bachelier's random walk and Fama's efficient markets.

Stochastic calculus — pricing the bet as the clock ticks

Bachelier theorised the random walk in 1900; Norbert Wiener (USA) proved such a continuous path exists in the 1920s — pure mathematics still calls it the Wiener process — and Kiyosi Itô (Japan) built a calculus on those jagged paths (Itô's lemma). Black, Scholes & Merton then used it in 1973 to price a contract that settles at 1 or 0 — a binary option, which is exactly a prediction-market contract — showing how volatility and the shrinking time to expiry move its value. These are the dynamics behind the pm-AMM.

Convex duality — why a market maker can't be bankrupted

An automated market maker is a convex cost function $C(\mathbf{q})$ over the outstanding shares, with prices read off as its gradient:

$$p_i=\frac{\partial C}{\partial q_i}.$$

Strict convexity does the work: every purchase lifts the price, the prices stay a valid probability vector, and the operator's worst-case loss is bounded by the range of $C$ ($b\log n$ for LMSR) — the same convex object whether you read it as a cost function or as a proper scoring rule.

Information theory & maximum entropy — paying for what you tell the market

LMSR uses logarithms because a market is an information-processing machine and the language of information is Shannon entropy; Edwin Jaynes's maximum-entropy principle even hands the maker its prior when it knows only part of the picture. Proper scoring rules are the dual: a trader who moves the market from a distribution $Q$ to $P$ is paid, in expectation, the information injected — the Kullback–Leibler divergence

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\parallel Q)=\sum_{x}P(x)\log\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)},$$

which is exactly a log-wealth bettor's growth rate against the old price (the kelly primitive) and the regret of the log score. Truth-telling and growth-optimal betting are one convex geometry.

VAR is reviewing (contested citizenships & disallowed goals)

  • Australia's captain holds three passports. George Julius was born in Norwich, England 🇬🇧; raised and engineering-schooled in New Zealand (Canterbury College); and built the totalisator in Sydney — which he first installed back in Auckland. England claims the birth and New Zealand the upbringing and the debut, but the ruling on the field stands: with the career, the knighthood, and the company all in Sydney, Julius plays for Australia.
  • William Vickrey — born in Victoria, British Columbia 🇨🇦; naturalised American; the only economics Nobel laureate born in BC. Played his whole career for 🇺🇸.
  • Philip Tetlock (Toronto 🇨🇦) and Yiling Chen (China 🇨🇳) also line up for the USA; Justin Wolfers was born in Papua New Guinea and raised in Sydney, playing for Australia.
  • Joseph Oller was Catalan (born in Terrassa 🇪🇸); de Finetti was born in Innsbruck; Daniel Bernoulli in Groningen; Wald in Cluj. Birthplace and shirt rarely agree.
  • Vitalik Buterin's AMM goal is disallowed. He suggested and popularised running on-chain exchanges like prediction markets, but invented no market mechanism and did not originate x·y=k. The credit reads Hanson (LMSR) → Gnosis (Köppelmann, constant-product origination) → Uniswap (Adams), with Bancor first to deploy.
  • The perpetual swap was scored from offshore (BitMEX). The Policy Analysis Market was a market on Middle-East macro indicators, not — as widely misreported — a market on assassinations; we score the design, not the headline.
