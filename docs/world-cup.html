A World Cup of Prediction Markets

Who actually built this stuff? An unscientific league table of national contributions to market mechanisms — whimsical in its rankings, sober in its facts. Every claim links to a real source; the medal count does not.

The final: 🇦🇺 Australia vs 🇺🇸 USA

The two heavyweights split the sport cleanly between hardware and theory.

Australia built the machine. The engineer George Julius patented an automatic totalisator in 1909 and installed the first at Ellerslie in 1913; his Automatic Totalisators Ltd then exported room-sized mechanical computers of shafts and adders that aggregated bets from hundreds of windows and recomputed the live odds in real time — a working analog computer that, as Brian Conlon argues, was plausibly the world's first large-scale, multi-user, real-time computing system, decades before the electronic kind. They turned a betting pool into a computer.

America wrote the rules and never stopped trading. The theory: Brier's proper score out of the Weather Bureau (1950), Kelly's growth-optimal betting at Bell Labs (1956), Vickrey's second-price auction (1961), and Hanson's logarithmic market scoring rule and futarchy. The markets: organised election betting on the Curb and in Broadway hotels from the 1860s to the 1940s (so accurate the mid-October favourite won 11 of 15 elections, 1884–1940), the Iowa Electronic Markets (1988), the short-lived DARPA Policy Analysis Market (2003), and the on-chain era of Uniswap and the perpetual swap.

Verdict. Australia has the single greatest artefact; America has the deepest bench, from Bell Labs to the blockchain. Call it a draw and send it to penalties — or note that the first tote was actually plugged in across the Tasman, and award the assist to New Zealand.

Contested citizenships (VAR is reviewing)

  • Joseph Oller was Catalan, born in Terrassa — so 🇪🇸 Spain lodges a protest over France's pari-mutuel goal.
  • Vitalik Buterin is Russian-born and Canadian-raised; the AMM assist has dual nationality.
  • The perpetual swap launched at BitMEX, incorporated in the Seychelles and run from Hong Kong 🇭🇰 — the most offshore goal in the tournament.
  • The Policy Analysis Market is often miscalled a market to bet on assassinations; it wasn't — it was a market on Middle-East macro indicators, killed within a day of a 2003 press conference. We score the design, not the headline.
